REACH

Project Overview

We all need the advice of an expert sometimes, and how-to videos don’t always cut it. Focusing on mental health, the app takes on a holistic approach in the area of mental health and provides a way to reach out for experts in these fields – be it exercise, food, meditation or psychological help.

My Role

Sole UX/UI Designer

Tools

Figma, Illustrator, Pen and Paper

1. Understand

Possible Problems

  • People may have trouble finding an expert for their problem area
  • Apps often don’t allow users to keep track on their specific struggles
  • Mental health apps often only focus on one aspect and don’t allow the user to connect with an expert
  • Beginners in any field might need access to more information tailored to their needs that apps don’t take into consideration

Problem Statement

As a user I want help from the app how to better my mental health, be it by matching me with a suitable expert or giving me useful tips/a mental health plan, so that I can feel better without having added stress.

Potential Solution

An app that allows people to create a profile with their specific need to better their mental health as well as a simple intuitive way to connect with an expert and allows them to keep track of their struggles to share with their expert.

Competitive Analysis

To get a better understanding of what’s in the market, I analyzed different competitors such as TalkSpace and Headspace to understand which existing user needs are already met and where a potential market gap could exist. It also allowed me to observe best practices and which mistakes to avoid.

Strengths

  • easy and cheaper alternative to normal therapy
  • getting help fast anytime
  • 3rd party vetting for therapists
  • additional human based support by helping the user find suitable therapist

Opportunities

  • expansion into B2B as well as Psychiatry
  • pushind the holistic approach for users

Weaknesses

  • text-based chat could lead to misunderstandings - talking about issues without inflection and facial expressions
  • too impersonal - therapists sometimes don't remember conversations
  • not as in-depth as in-office therapy

Threats

  • online therapy platforms that focus on video chats
  • traditional in person therapy

2. Observe

User Interviews

It was important to actually talk to my users to be able to get a better feel for them. To do so, I interviewed with three people to see what their experiences regarding mental health and possibly other apps have been and to be able to collect qualitative data.

What I wanted to find out:

People's habits concerning mental health

People’s opinions towards online consulting (i.e. talking to an expert online)

Experiences with other mental health apps

Insights

  • Most of the interviewees stated that talking to other people helps them cope with their mental health and potential problems
  • Having a recommendation for an expert is highly valued
  • To keep motivated and continue their habits, most people preferred having to go to a course on a set time - something they referred to as having an outside pressure
  • Most like to consult with an expert when learning a new practice for their mental health

3. User Personas and Flows

Based on my previous research, I created two user personas - Ashley, who is more of a casual user and just wants to use the app to de-stress, and Brooke, who is interested in having the full-on experience of getting all the recommendations for her mental health program and experts in those fields.

I always kept these two personas and their interests in mind while creating my prototypes later on.

4. Ideate

From quickly sketching out screens with pen and paper to create a concept of the app, turning them into low-fidelity wireframes and eventually a mid-fidelity prototype to test with users and finally a high-fidelity version, I went through several iterations of my app. Having the possibility to test these versions with people improving the functionality as well as the visualization of the app, I was able to come up with a solution based on user research.

5. Test

Usability Testing

To be able to make sure my prototype worked the way I intended it to, I wanted to test it with real users. I did two rounds of usability tests.

In a first round, I tested my mid-fidelity prototype with 6 participants. Overall, the participants found the app easy to use and logically structured. However, analyzing the data, there were some issues that consistently appeared with all or most of the participants. In a second round, I tested my high-fidelity amongst my peers. With their feedback, I could eliminate some minor details.

Top 3 issues

  • Users didn’t really understand what the app was about
  • There was not enough whitespace
  • Users felt lost at one point of the onboarding

1 and 2

Because people didn’t understand what the app was about quickly (with the exception of reading all the information carefully), I decided to introduce a banner at the top which should feature the main goal of the app. Additionally, if users wanted to read more, I presented what the app can do in a more visually pleasing way. Furthermore, after the peer review I introduced more white space on the screen to make it more easily readable and less text heavy.

7. Learnings

During this process, I’ve learnt that it is important rather than wanting to have a perfect screen or prototype from the beginning to keep a user-centric approach and to test faster with potential users, so that I can be confident that my work is something potential users want. I’ve also enjoyed interacting with my peers to get new ideas and impulses.
